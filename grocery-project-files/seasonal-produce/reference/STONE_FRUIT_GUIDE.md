# California Stone Fruit Variety Guide

## Why This Matters

Most grocery store stone fruit is bred for shipping durability, not flavor. The best stone fruit varieties have a brutally short window -- sometimes just 10 days -- and are only available at farmers markets. Knowing what to look for and when transforms summer fruit from disappointing to transcendent.

---

## Peaches

### Yellow Peaches (Central Valley, May--Sep, peak Jul--Aug)

- **O'Henry** (early Aug): The benchmark yellow freestone. Rich, balanced acid-sweet, firm but juicy. Available at both farmers markets and better grocery stores.
- **Elegant Lady** (Jul): Dominates grocery stores. Bred for machine harvest and shipping -- looks perfect but often mealy and bland compared to market varieties.
- **Suncrest** (mid-Jul): Legendary flavor -- widely considered the best yellow peach ever bred. Nearly extinct commercially because it's too soft to ship. Farmers market only.
- **Cal Red** (mid-Aug): Deep red skin, firm yellow flesh. Better than average grocery peach.
- General rule: The prettier and more uniform a peach looks, the more likely it was bred for shipping over flavor.

### White Peaches (Central Valley, Jun--Aug, peak Jun--Jul)

- **Babcock** (mid-Jul): The classic California white peach. Floral, honey-sweet, low acid. The skin is so thin it's nearly transparent.
- **Snow series** (various): Snow Beauty, Snow King -- consistently sweet whites from Zaiger Genetics.
- **Arctic whites** (various): Bred by Zaiger, available at farmers markets across the season.
- White peaches are sweeter and more floral than yellow, with lower acid. More delicate -- buy at farmers markets for best quality.

### Donut/Saturn Peaches (Central Valley, May--Aug, peak Jun--Jul)

- **Galaxy**: The main commercial variety. Flat shape, melting texture, very sweet with a hint of almond. Increasingly available at grocery stores.
- Flat shape means the pit is tiny relative to flesh volume -- good value.

### Import Peaches (Chile, Dec--Mar)

Chilean peaches suffer from long cold-chain transport plus being picked from shipping-bred varieties. High mealiness risk. Generally not worth buying -- spend the money on citrus instead.

## Nectarines

### Central Valley, Jun--Sep, peak Jul--Aug

- **Snow Queen** (late Jun): Widely considered the sweetest nectarine. White flesh, stunning flavor.
- **Fantasia** (late Jul): The reference standard yellow nectarine. Balanced sweet-tart.
- **Independence** (mid-Jul): Rich tangy-sweet flavor, reliable quality.
- **Arctic series** (white, farmers markets): Spans the whole season with different varieties. All excellent.
- Nectarines are genetically just smooth-skinned peaches -- not a peach-plum hybrid.

### Import Nectarines (Chile, Dec--Mar)

Same cold-chain issues as imported peaches. Plums and cherries import better than nectarines.

---

## Plums

### Central Valley, Jun--Oct, peak Jun--Jul

- **Santa Rosa** (mid-Jun): The iconic California plum. Luther Burbank creation. Tart skin, sweet amber flesh.
- **Satsuma** (Jul): Blood-red flesh throughout. Excellent for cooking and preserves.
- **Elephant Heart** (late Jul): Incredibly sweet, huge, heart-shaped. Too soft to ship -- farmers market treasure.
- **Black Amber** (Jul): Dark skin, pale flesh. Common in grocery stores, decent quality.

### Import Plums (Chile, Dec--Apr, peak Jan--Feb)

Chilean plums are more acceptable than imported peaches -- plums handle the cold supply chain better. Quality is decent for winter fruit.

---

## Pluots

### Central Valley, Jun--Sep, peak Jul--Aug

Created by Floyd Zaiger of Zaiger Genetics in Modesto, CA. Plum-apricot hybrids -- typically 70-75% plum genetics.

- **Flavor King** (mid-Aug): Widely considered THE best pluot. Incredibly complex -- sweet, spicy, with a richness that's hard to describe. Deep purple-red.
- **Dapple Dandy / Dinosaur Egg** (mid-Aug to mid-Sep): Marbled green-and-red skin, pink flesh. Sweet and juicy.
- **Flavor Queen** (mid-to-late Jul): Honey-sweet green/yellow pluot. Early-to-mid pluot season.
- **Flavor Grenade** (Aug--Sep): Green oblong shape, crunchy texture, grape-like sweetness.
- Pluots typically measure 2-5 brix higher than plums. The sugar content is genuinely remarkable.

---

## Apricots

### Central Valley, May--Jul, peak Jun

- **Blenheim / Royal** (late Jun): THE gold standard. Complex sweet-tart with honeysuckle notes. On the Slow Food Ark of Taste. Season is often just 10 days in late June at farmers markets. One of the most seasonal fruits in California.
- **Patterson**: Dominates commercial production (roughly low-80% share in cited UC ANR summaries). Bred for machine harvest and canning. Bland compared to Blenheim but widely available.
- **Tilton**: Another commercial variety. Acceptable for cooking/preserving, mediocre fresh.
- The gap between a grocery store Patterson and a farmers market Blenheim is possibly the largest quality gap in all of produce.

---

## Cherries

### Central Valley + Sacramento Valley, Apr--Jun, peak May--Jun

CA cherry season is only about 6 weeks. Premium but brief.

- **Bing** (late May--early Jun): THE reference cherry. Deep mahogany, sweet, firm.
- **Rainier** (late May--Jun): Highest sugar content of common varieties. Yellow-red blush. Premium pricing.
- **Brooks** (mid-Apr): The earliest good cherry. Slightly softer than Bing.
- **Coral Champagne**: White/blush cherry, milder sweetness.

### Import Cherries (Chile, Nov--Feb, peak Dec--Jan)

Chilean cherries are surprisingly decent -- cherries are hardier in cold storage than peaches or nectarines. Worth buying in winter, especially for cooking.

## Buying Guide: General Rules

1. **Smell the stem end.** If it doesn't smell like anything, it won't taste like anything.
2. **Avoid imports for peaches and nectarines.** The cold chain destroys texture. Cherries and plums import much better.
3. **Farmers market varieties are a different food category.** The gap between a shipped Elegant Lady peach and a market Suncrest is like iceberg lettuce vs. fresh garden greens.
4. **"Ripe" at the store usually means "2 days out."** Buy stone fruit slightly firm and ripen at room temperature, stem-end down.
5. **Never refrigerate unripe stone fruit.** Cold storage causes mealiness. Ripen first, then refrigerate to slow decay.
6. **Late July through mid-August is the golden window.** This is when the highest-quality varieties from all stone fruit categories overlap.
